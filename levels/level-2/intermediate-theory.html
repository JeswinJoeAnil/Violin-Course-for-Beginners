Intermediate Harmony

Expanding beyond the triad: Seventh chords, minor scales, and chromaticism.

Part 1

The Minor Scale Complex

Unlike major keys which have exactly one standard scale form, minor keys use a complex of three interconnected scales to satisfy harmonic and melodic needs.

🌿 Natural Minor (Aeolian)

The foundation. Uses only the notes of its relative major key. Example, A minor: A, B, C, D, E, F, G. Often lacks "pull" towards the tonic.

🎹 Harmonic Minor

Created by raising the 7th scale degree of the natural minor by a half-step. This creates a leading tone back to the tonic. Example: A, B, C, D, E, F, G#.

🎶 Melodic Minor

Created by raising both the 6th and 7th degrees ascending, and returning them to natural descending. This fixes the awkward augmented 2nd jump found in the harmonic minor scale.

Part 2

Seventh Chords

🎸 Adding the 7th

By stacking one more third on top of a triad, we create a 7th chord. In diatonic harmony, this yields several types of 7th chords that create forward motion and tension.

Type Formula Example (C Root) Resolution
Major 7th 1 - 3 - 5 - 7 C - E - G - B Stable, restful color.
Dominant 7th 1 - 3 - 5 - b7 C - E - G - Bb High tension, resolves strictly to Target Tonic.
Minor 7th 1 - b3 - 5 - b7 C - Eb - G - Bb Smooth, jazzy sound. Functions as ii chord.
Half-Diminished 7th 1 - b3 - b5 - b7 C - Eb - Gb - Bb Often used as ii in a minor key progression.
