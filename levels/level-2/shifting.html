Level II: Intermediate Skills → 2.2 Shifting

Shifting Positions

Navigating the fingerboard safely and silently using Yost and Sevcik principles.

Part 1

The Anatomy of a Shift

A shift is not a jump; it is a controlled slide. The hand moves as a single unit—thumb, knuckles, and fingers together. According to the Carl Flesch system, you must release all finger pressure to a surface "harmonic" touch during the travel time.

Part 2

Types of Shifts

🚂 Same-Finger Shift

The simplest shift. Start on the 1st finger (B on A string), release pressure, slide the 1st finger up, and press down when it reaches the target note (e.g., D in 3rd position).

🔄 Guide-Finger Shift (Classical)

Shifting from one finger to another. Rule: The finger playing the FIRST note slides to the new position, THEN the new finger drops. Ex: 1st finger slides, then 3rd finger drops.

Part 3

The Yost System Exercises

Gaylord Yost's shifting system emphasizes that the thumb must anticipate the shift. The thumb moves slightly ahead of the hand when shifting up, and releases instantly when shifting down.

🐢

Slow Practice (Sevcik Op. 8)

Practice shifting on a single string with a metronome at ♩=40. Make the slide deliberately slow and audible (a glissando) to train your ear to measure the distance. Over weeks, speed up the shift until the slide is inaudible.

🪶

Ghosting the Shift

Before executing a tricky shift in repertoire, practice "ghosting" it: release the bow weight so the shift makes almost no sound, ensuring completely relaxed left-hand mechanics.
