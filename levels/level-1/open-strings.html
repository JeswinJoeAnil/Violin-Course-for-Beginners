The Four Open Strings

Discovering the Core Resonances of the Violin

The Basics

G, D, A, E The String Family

The violin has four strings, each with a distinct thickness, tension, and tone color. From lowest to highest pitch, they are G, D, A, and E.

🎻

G String (Lowest)

The thickest and lowest-pitched string. It has a rich, deep, and warm timbre, often compared to the human alto voice. It requires slightly more arm weight to speak clearly.

✨

D String

A mellow, smooth-sounding string that bridges the gap between the dark G string and the brighter A string. It's the core of the violin's middle range.

🎼

A String

The standard reference string. Orchestras always tune to a concert A (440 Hz or 442 Hz) played on this string. It is bright, clear, and focused.

💎

E String (Highest)

The thinnest string, capable of brilliant, piercing, and lyrical soaring melodies. Extra care must be taken with contact point to prevent whistling.

Fundamentals

🔊 Why "Open" Strings?

An open string means you play the string without pressing any finger on the fingerboard. The full length of the string vibrates freely, producing its natural tuned pitch. These are your reference pitches — everything you play is measured against them.

Open strings have a distinctive resonant, full, and projecting tone because nothing dampens the vibration. Many professional violinists deliberately use open strings in passages for this extra ring, even when a fingered note on a neighboring string would produce the same pitch.

Tuning

🎼 Tuning the Open Strings

The number after each letter refers to the octave. The violin spans over two octaves on open strings alone.

🎵 Tuning Sequence

The A string (440 Hz) is always tuned first — 440 Hz is the internationally agreed concert pitch. Orchestras tune from the oboe's A.

  1. Tune A first (to 440 Hz reference)
  2. Tune D against A
  3. Tune G against D
  4. Tune E against A
✨ Sympathetic Vibration

When two adjacent strings are perfectly in tune, you'll hear them ring together — this is called sympathetic vibration. It's the most reliable sign of accurate tuning, more reliable than most digital tuners.

Perfect fifths check: Bow two adjacent open strings simultaneously. If they produce a clean, pure interval without beating or wavering, they are in tune.

The Fingerboard

🖐️ Understanding the Fingerboard

The violin fingerboard is a smooth, fretless piece of ebony glued to the neck. There are no markings, no frets, no guides — just a flat surface. This is what makes the violin harder than a guitar but also infinitely expressive. You can slide between pitches, create vibrato, and play microtones.

Key insight: The 4th finger on any string produces the exact same pitch as the next open string. 4th finger on G = open D. 4th finger on D = open A. This is a built-in tuning check you should use every practice session.

Positions

📍 What Positions Are

A position refers to where your left hand sits along the fingerboard. It is always named after where your 1st finger lands.

  • First position (where every beginner starts): 1st finger is about 1 inch from the nut. On the A string it plays B4.
  • Second position: Hand slides up slightly, 1st finger now plays C5 on the A string.
  • Third position: 1st finger plays D5 on the A string.

The higher the position, the higher the notes. As you shift up, the spaces between fingers get smaller because the vibrating string length shortens. Beginners stay entirely in first position for months — the first three Suzuki books, countless folk pieces, and baroque works all stay in first position.

Contact Point

🎯 The Sounding Point (In Depth)

Why this matters: No matter how accurately your left hand places notes, a wrong sounding point will destroy the tone. Intonation and sounding point work together — you cannot have one without the other.
🔬 What It Is

The sounding point (also called contact point or punto d'appoggio) is the precise location where your bow hair meets the string, measured as the distance between the bridge and the end of the fingerboard. This strip — roughly 4–6 cm wide — is your entire tonal palette.

Near the bridge: the string is stiff and resistant. It requires more bow speed and weight, but produces a rich, complex, projecting tone with many overtones.

Near the fingerboard: the string is flexible and compliant. Little pressure needed, but the tone is thin and hollow with fewer overtones. This is why beginners who drift toward the fingerboard sound scratchy.

Your goal as a beginner: find and stay consistently in the primary zone (2.5–4 cm from bridge). Everything else comes later.

⚙️ The Four Variables That Control Tone

The sounding point is part of a four-variable system. Professional violinists manage all four simultaneously:

  1. Bow Speed — how fast the bow moves. Faster speed = lighter, clearer tone. Near the bridge you must increase speed to prevent scratching.
  2. Bow Weight — natural arm weight (not pressure) transmitted through a relaxed hand. Weight without sufficient speed = crunch and scratch.
  3. Bow Distribution — frog (heavy, near your hand), middle, or tip (lighter). Tip near bridge requires deliberate arm weight.
  4. String Crossing Angle — each string sits at a different height. The sounding point distance must stay consistent as the arm rises and falls.

Moving the sounding point closer to the bridge requires you to simultaneously increase bow speed AND arm weight. Changing one variable without adjusting the others breaks the sound.

Connection

🔗 Why This Affects Finger Placement

👂 Intonation Through Resonance

When you play a fingered note at the correct sounding point, the violin body and other strings sympathetically vibrate if the note is in tune. If your bow is near the fingerboard, the tone is too weak to hear this resonance — you're effectively practicing intonation blind.

🧠 Building Muscle Memory Together

Your left hand and right arm are not independent systems — they're learned together. If you practice finger placements while bowing lazily at the wrong sounding point, the muscle memory includes the wrong bow location. When you eventually correct the bow, the left hand will feel unfamiliar. Always train both together from day one.

Watch Out

⚠️ Common Mistakes

🎯

Drifting Toward the Fingerboard

The most universal beginner habit. The string near the fingerboard is easier to set in motion — but the resulting tone is thin and scratchy. Fix: practice with a mirror placed to your side so you can watch the bow lane.

↔️

Inconsistent Lane

The bow drifts closer to bridge during up-bows and toward fingerboard during down-bows. Creates fluctuating tone. Fix: draw two pencil lines 3 cm apart on paper and keep the bow between them.

😬

Too Much Pressure, Too Little Speed

Results in a horrible crunching surface scratch. Fix: whenever you move toward the bridge, immediately increase bow speed. Weight without speed = crunch.

🔄

Not Adjusting When Crossing Strings

The sounding point distance from the bridge should stay the same on all four strings, but the arm height changes. Fix: slow string crossings in a mirror, keeping the bow lane constant.

Daily Practice

✅ What to Practice Right Now

🎯 Daily Sounding Point Drill (5 Minutes)

This drill is used in conservatory foundation training. Do it before every practice session.

  1. Play a single open A string with a slow, full bow stroke
  2. Start with the bow at the fingerboard edge and very slowly move it toward the bridge over 4–5 strokes, listening to how the tone transforms
  3. Find the exact point where the tone becomes richest, fullest, and most resonant — this is your primary zone for today
  4. Mark that zone mentally, and begin your left-hand exercises only after you've found it
  5. During all finger placement drills, keep returning to this zone whenever you notice the tone weakening
🎻

Tune Daily with Resonance

Don't just trust a tuner — train your ear to hear the sympathetic ringing between adjacent strings. This is the foundation of all intonation work.

🔍

4th Finger Test

Play 4th finger → open next string on every string. They must match. If they don't, adjust. This is your most reliable daily intonation check.

🪞

Mirror Practice

Watch your bow in a mirror while playing. It should travel in a perfectly straight horizontal line, parallel to the bridge, 2–4 cm away. Every stroke.

👁️‍🗨️

Eyes-Closed Sounding Point

Play an open string with eyes closed and move the bow closer then farther from the bridge. Listen to how the tone changes. This trains your ear faster than anything else.

The One-Sentence Rule: A correct sounding point turns practice into progress — a wrong one turns practice into reinforcing bad habits, no matter how many hours you put in.
