Time Signatures

Understanding Rhythm, Beats, and Meter in Music

The Meter

Decoding the Fractions

At the beginning of every piece of music, right after the clef and key signature, you will see two numbers stacked vertically, like a fraction. This is the Time Signature.

⬆ The Top Number

The top number tells you how many beats are in each measure (or bar).

For example, if the top number is 4, there are 4 beats per measure. You count: "1-2-3-4, 1-2-3-4".

⬇ The Bottom Number

The bottom number tells you which type of note counts as one beat.

  • 4 = Quarter note (♩) gets the beat
  • 8 = Eighth note (♪) gets the beat
  • 2 = Half note gets the beat
Common Meters

Famous Time Signatures

🚶

4/4 (Common Time)

The most popular time signature. 4 beats per measure, quarter note gets the beat. Often indicated with a "C" instead of numbers. Used in most rock, pop, and classical symphonies.

💃

3/4 (Waltz Time)

3 beats per measure, quarter note gets the beat. It has a strong 1st beat and weaker 2nd and 3rd beats (ONE, two, three). Classical dances and waltzes use this.

🪖

2/4 (March Time)

2 beats per measure, quarter note gets the beat. Strong alternating left-right marching feel (LEFT, right, LEFT, right).

⛵

6/8 (Compound Time)

6 eighth notes per measure, but it is typically felt in 2 sweeping beats (groups of 3). It creates a rolling, lilting feel common in Irish jigs or sailing shanties.

Practice

Rhythm Pyramid

Before applying rhythm to the violin, practice clapping the basic subdivisions of 4/4 time with a metronome at 60 BPM:

Name Notes per 4/4 Measure How to Count
Whole Note 1 "1 (hold 2, 3, 4)"
Half Notes 2 "1 (hold 2), 3 (hold 4)"
Quarter Notes 4 "1, 2, 3, 4"
Eighth Notes 8 "1-and, 2-and, 3-and, 4-and"
Sixteenth Notes 16 "1-e-and-a, 2-e-and-a..."
