🎯 How to Use This System

Work through each level sequentially. Do not rush to advance — mastery at each stage is the foundation of all future progress. The Galamian methodology emphasises that technical difficulties must be isolated, practised slowly, then gradually accelerated using a metronome in systematic rhythm variations.

  • Daily practice is non-negotiable — even 30 focused minutes outperforms 2 hours of mindless repetition
  • Record yourself weekly; the ear cannot hear what the mind is not trained to notice
  • Integrate theory alongside technique — understanding what you play transforms muscle memory into musical intelligence
  • Follow the Practice System tab for structured daily and weekly routines at every level
  • Use the Ear Training module daily — 15 minutes minimum

Setting Up: Instrument & Body

🎻 Holding the Violin
Shoulder Rest
Holding Posture
Left Hand Position

Step-by-Step Setup (Galamian Method)

  1. Shoulder rest: Attach and adjust so violin rests naturally on collarbone — no tension in the neck or jaw
  2. Chin rest: Left jaw rests on the chin rest — NOT the chin itself in most cases
  3. Left arm: Bring the violin up with the left arm supporting the scroll; the instrument should be roughly parallel to the floor
  4. Scroll direction: Point the scroll toward your left front corner (not directly to the side)
  5. Left thumb: Relaxed, slightly bent, touching the neck opposite the first finger — never gripping
  6. Fingers curved: All fingers approach the strings from above, with curved knuckles (as if holding a small ball)
Critical check: If you feel tension in your neck, jaw, or left shoulder — stop immediately and reset. All tension is the enemy of technique and will cause injury.
🏹 The Bow Hold (Franco-Belgian)

Galamian Bow Grip

  1. Thumb: Curved, placed on the underside of the stick opposite the middle finger — never stiff
  2. Index finger: Rests on the stick at the middle joint (not the tip), angled toward the frog
  3. Middle & ring fingers: Draped naturally over the stick, evenly spaced
  4. Pinky: Curved and placed on top of the stick — acts as a counterbalance
  5. Wrist: Slightly flexible, never rigid

Common Errors

  • Thumb straightening (locks the whole arm)
  • Gripping with all fingers (prevents tone shaping)
  • Collapsing pinky (lose control at the tip)
  • Wrist too high or too stiff
Exercise 1.1 — Bow Arm

The "Windshield Wiper" Warm-Up

Hold the bow at the balance point with your full bow grip. With the bow OFF the violin, practice slow full-arm swings from frog to tip and back. Focus only on maintaining the curved thumb and relaxed fingers. Do this 5 minutes daily for the first month before touching strings.

Exercise 1.2 — Left Hand

Finger Placement Drill (Sevcik Op.1, Part 1)

  • Place all four fingers on the A string in first position: B–C#–D–E
  • Lift each finger slowly, one at a time, keeping the others down
  • Each finger should fall with a light "tap" — not a press
  • The knuckles must remain visibly arched throughout
  • Repeat on D, G, and E strings — 10 min per practice session

Contact Point (Sounding Point)

Draw the bow between the bridge and the fingerboard — approximately 2–4 cm from the bridge. Closer to the bridge = brighter, more projection. Closer to the fingerboard = softer, more hollow. This is called the sounding point or contact point.

🎼 Tone Production Trinity

Every element of violin tone depends on balancing three variables simultaneously:

  • BOW SPEED Fast bow = louder, lighter tone
  • BOW PRESSURE More weight = richer, fuller tone (use arm weight, not force)
  • CONTACT PT. Distance from bridge (nearer = brighter)
Galamian's Law: Pressure and speed must always increase together. Slow bow + heavy pressure = crushed, scratchy tone. Fast bow + no pressure = thin, airy tone.
Exercise 1.3 — Tone Building

Long Bow Exercise on Open Strings

  • Use a full bow stroke on each open string: 4 slow counts DOWN, 4 counts UP
  • Listen for a singing, clear, consistent tone — no scratching, no whistle
  • Use arm weight (let gravity help you) — do not press with the hand
  • Progress to 8-count strokes, then 12-count strokes
  • Record yourself and compare the tone quality before and after
  • Practice: 10 minutes daily, all 4 strings

What Is a Staff?

A staff (also called a stave) is the grid that all written music sits on. It consists of 5 horizontal lines and the 4 spaces between them. Each line and space represents a different pitch — the higher up the staff, the higher the note sounds.

The Lines There are 5 lines, counted from the bottom upward. On the treble clef, they are: E – G – B – D – F. A note sitting directly on a line has a line running through its centre.
The Spaces The 4 gaps between lines are also pitches. From bottom to top they spell: F – A – C – E — conveniently, the word "FACE". A note sitting in a space is nestled between two lines.
Ledger Lines When a note is too high or too low to fit on the 5 lines, we add short extra lines called ledger lines above or below the staff. The note sits on or between these, just like on the main staff. For example, middle C (C4) on the violin sits on a ledger line just below the staff.
The Clef The curling symbol at the far left is the treble clef (𝄞). It is the first thing written on every line of violin music. Its curl wraps around the second line from the bottom, telling you that line = G4. This is why it is also called the G clef. Once you know that one anchor note (G on line 2), you can name every other note by counting up or down the alphabet (A B C D E F G A…).
Reading Tip Don't try to memorise all notes at once. Start with the two memory tricks — "Every Good Boy Does Fine" for the lines and "FACE" for the spaces — and drill them daily until they are instant. Then add ledger lines one at a time.

2.1

Vibrato — Step-by-Step System

〜 The Galamian Vibrato Method

Vibrato is an oscillation of pitch produced by a rocking motion of the left hand. There are three types: arm vibrato, wrist vibrato, and finger vibrato. Galamian taught that the best vibrato combines all three, with the arm initiating the motion.

Stage 1: Arm Rocking (Weeks 1–2)

  1. Place the violin on your knee (not under chin) or on a table, scroll pointing away
  2. Place 2nd finger lightly on the A string at C#
  3. Rock the entire forearm forward and backward — from the elbow
  4. The finger should roll slightly (not slide) as the arm moves
  5. Goal: smooth, continuous wave — not nervous shaking

Stage 2: Under the Chin (Weeks 3–4)

  1. Transfer the arm-rocking motion to normal playing position
  2. Start on 2nd finger — it is the most stable
  3. Play a long open A, then switch to 2nd finger C# with vibrato
  4. Metronome: start at ♩=60, rocking twice per beat
  5. Progress to 3 rocks/beat, then 4, then free/musical
Vibrato Exercise — Daily 10 min

The Metronome Rocking Drill

Set metronome to ♩=60. Place 3rd finger on G string (Bb). Rock arm vibrato 2 oscillations per beat for 2 minutes. Then increase to 3 per beat for 2 minutes. Then 4 per beat. Finally, turn off metronome and let the vibrato flow naturally. Repeat on all four strings, all four fingers. This is the complete foundation of the Galamian vibrato system.

🔄 Shifting Technique

The Galamian Shift

  1. Guide finger: Keep the shifting finger lightly on the string as you move — it "slides" the thumb and hand together
  2. Thumb leads: The thumb moves slightly before the fingers reach the new position
  3. No grip: Loosen the thumb and palm contact before shifting — never shift with tension
  4. Arrival note: The destination finger lands with a gentle placement, not a slam
  5. Practice slowly: Use the metronome at ♩=40 initially
Flesch Rule: Practice shifts in isolation — play the departure note, shift (with a brief slide), arrive on the target note. The slide should be inaudible in performance.
Exercise 2.1 — Shifting

Isolated Shift Drill (Galamian / Yost Method)

  • On the A string: play A (1st finger, 1st position). Shift to 3rd position (1st finger = C#)
  • Listen for the shift — it should be smooth, not jerky
  • Bow continues through the shift without a pause
  • Practice 1st→3rd, 1st→5th, 3rd→5th, 1st→7th
  • Then practice the same shifts on all four strings
  • Resource: Yost "Shifting" Exercises / Dont Op.38
2.3

Bowing Techniques — Expanded

Détaché
SEPARATE BOW STROKES
Smooth, separate strokes with no accent between notes. The foundation of all bowing. Full bow, middle bow, or short détaché depending on musical context.
Martelé
HAMMERED STROKE
Each note has a sharp accent at the beginning then releases. Produced by applying pressure before the stroke, then releasing as the bow moves. Strong, assertive character.
Legato / Slur
CONNECTED NOTES
Multiple notes played in one bow direction. The left hand does all the work; the bow flows smoothly. Essential for lyrical, singing passages.
Staccato
SEPARATED SHORT NOTES
Short, detached notes with silence between. Can be on- or off-string. On-string staccato (solid) vs flying staccato (advanced). Begin with on-string.
Spiccato
BOUNCING BOW (ADVANCED)
The bow bounces off the string naturally at medium speed. Played at the balance point of the bow. Introduced at late-intermediate stage (see Level III).
Louré / Portato
PULSED LEGATO
Multiple notes in one bow but with slight separations / swellings. Creates a gentle, pulsing, connected sound. Common in slow movements.

Intermediate Theory Module

⭕ Circle of Fifths
C (0) → G (1#) → D (2#) → A (3#) → E (4#) → B (5#)
C (0) → F (1♭) → B♭ (2♭) → E♭ (3♭) → A♭ (4♭) → D♭ (5♭)
Moving clockwise = add sharps. Moving counter-clockwise = add flats.

Practical Application for Violinists

  • G Major (1 sharp: F#) — most common violin key
  • D Major (2 sharps: F#, C#) — brilliant, open string resonance
  • A Major (3 sharps) — warm, rich on violin
  • E Major (4 sharps) — bright, projecting

3.1

Advanced Bowing — Off-String Techniques

🏹 Spiccato — The Bouncing Bow

Finding the Spiccato Point

  1. Hold the bow in the air at the balance point (roughly middle-lower)
  2. Tap the bow on the A string — it will bounce naturally
  3. That bounce point is your spiccato zone (varies per bow)
  4. To control: a smaller arm motion = smaller, controlled bounce
  5. Never force the bounce — let gravity do the work

Development Stages

  1. On-string staccato fast — très détaché at ♩=120+
  2. Collé — small "pinch and release" strokes at the frog
  3. Natural spiccato — let the bow naturally lift at high speeds
  4. Controlled spiccato — consciously guide the bounce height

Sautillé (Advanced)

A faster, smaller spiccato where the bow barely leaves the string. Occurs naturally at high bow speeds. Not controlled consciously — it emerges from a correctly coordinated détaché at the correct bow speed.

Drill — Spiccato

The "Rain Drop" Exercise

Play open A string with short, light strokes at the balance point. Start on the string (détaché), then gradually speed up until the bow begins to naturally lift. Sustain that speed for 30 seconds. Then slow back down to controlled. Practice 10 min daily until spiccato is reliable.

Bow closer to bridge than normal. Use very light bow pressure — the harmonic is destroyed by too much weight.

🌟 Artificial Harmonics

Press the root note firmly with the 1st finger. Touch (not press) a perfect 4th above with the 4th finger. The resulting pitch is 2 octaves above the stopped note.

Artificial Harmonic Formula

  • Stopped note: 1st finger (firm)
  • Touch note: 4th finger, P4 above (light)
  • Result: Pitch = 2 octaves above stopped note
  • Example: 1st finger on E, 4th finger on A → sounds E two octaves above
Famous use: Ravel's Tzigane, Saint-Saëns' Introduction & Rondo Capriccioso, Rimsky-Korsakov's Scheherazade
3.3

Double Stops — Thirds, Sixths, Octaves

🎵 Double Stop System (Galamian Scale System)
Double Stops in Thirds — On the Strings
Hover a note dot — crimson = lower note, green = upper note (3rd above)

Double stops are two notes played simultaneously on adjacent strings. They are the foundation of polyphonic violin playing and require perfect intonation on both notes simultaneously.

Thirds

The most common double stop. Fingers on adjacent strings, a third apart. In D major on D and A strings: D+F#, E+G, F#+A, G+B, A+C#, B+D, C#+E, D+F#.

Drill — Thirds

D Major Scale in Thirds

Play the D major scale in thirds slowly (♩=50). Listen carefully — both notes must be equally in tune. Record yourself. Increase tempo weekly by 4 BPM. Reference: Flesch Scale System, Carl Flesch.

Octaves & Sixths

Octaves: 1st and 4th finger on adjacent strings, one octave apart. Requires a wide hand frame. Strong bow weight with a firm left-hand grip.

Sixths: Slightly easier than thirds in some keys. 1st and 3rd finger on adjacent strings. Used extensively in Romantic-era violin writing.

Reference

Sevcik Op.9 — Double Stops

The definitive double stop studies. Begin with Part 1 (thirds), then sixths (Part 2), then octaves. One exercise from each section per practice session, mastered slowly before speeding up.

Advanced Theory — Counterpoint & Voice Leading

📐 Voice Leading Rules

The Four Rules of SATB Voice Leading

  1. No parallel 5ths or octaves — two voices moving in the same direction to a 5th or octave sounds hollow
  2. No voice crossing — keep soprano above alto, alto above tenor, tenor above bass
  3. Resolve leading tones — the 7th scale degree always resolves up by step to the tonic
  4. Contrary motion preferred — when the bass goes up, try to move the melody down

Cadence Types

  • Authentic (PAC/IAC): V → I (strongest possible ending)
  • Half: any chord → V (feels like a question)
  • Plagal: IV → I ("Amen" cadence)
  • Deceptive: V → vi (surprise — sounds unfinished)

4.1

Virtuoso Bow Techniques

⚡ Flying Staccato

A series of staccato notes in ONE bow direction (usually up-bow), with the bow barely leaving the string between each note. The wrist and fingers create each staccato impulse while the arm moves slowly in one direction.

Development Method

  1. Begin: 4 notes per bow at ♩=60 (up-bow)
  2. Increase to 8 notes, 12 notes, 16 notes
  3. Goal: 16+ rapid notes in one smooth bow
  4. Reference: Paganini Caprice No.1, Wieniawski Etude-Caprices
🔥 Ricochet / Jeté

The bow is thrown onto the string at the balance point and bounces multiple times automatically. Unlike spiccato (consciously bounced), ricochet is uncontrolled bouncing used for specific effects (e.g., 4, 6, or 8 notes from one drop).

How to Practice

  1. Drop the bow onto the A string from about 2cm height at the balance point
  2. It will bounce 4–6 times naturally — listen to the rhythm
  3. Control by adjusting the angle of the stick and the initial force
  4. Famous use: Paganini Caprice No.1, opening; Saint-Saëns Rondo Capriccioso
🎻 Left Hand Virtuoso Techniques

Left-Hand Pizzicato

Plucking the string with the left hand while bowing continues. Used in Paganini Caprices and many show pieces. Ring finger or 4th finger hooks under the string and plucks upward while other fingers hold bowed notes.

Col Legno

Striking the string with the wood of the bow stick rather than the hair. Produces a dry, percussive, ghost-like tone. Famous: Berlioz "Symphonie Fantastique" — the witches' dance.

Sul Ponticello / Sul Tasto

  • Sul ponticello: Bow very close to the bridge — produces a glassy, metallic, nasal sound (used for mysterious/eerie effects)
  • Sul tasto: Bow over the fingerboard — produces a warm, hollow, flute-like tone
  • Both are colour tools — used selectively for expressive contrast

Scordatura

Deliberate alternate tuning of one or more strings. E.g., Mahler Symphony No.4 (E string tuned up a tone), or Biber Mystery Sonatas. Creates unique colour and enables passages impossible in standard tuning.

✍️ Improvisation Foundations

Improvising over Chord Changes

  1. Learn the scale for each chord: Major chord = major scale/Ionian; Minor chord = Dorian or Aeolian; Dominant 7th = Mixolydian
  2. Target chord tones: Land on the root, 3rd, or 5th of each chord on strong beats
  3. Use passing tones: Connect chord tones with stepwise motion
  4. Call and response: Play a 2-bar phrase, leave 2 bars of silence, answer it
  5. Rhythm first: A rhythmically interesting simple melody beats a technically brilliant but rhythmically dull one
Start here: Improvise over a I–V–vi–IV loop (C–G–Am–F) using only the C major pentatonic scale (C D E G A). Add one note per week.

Counterpoint — Species System

📜 The Five Species of Counterpoint (Fux / Juilliard)

1st Species — Note against Note

One counterpoint note per cantus firmus note. Allowed intervals: unison, 3rd, 5th, 6th, octave. Forbidden: parallel 5ths, parallel octaves, dissonances.

2nd Species — 2:1 (Half notes)

Two notes against each cantus note. Dissonances allowed on weak beats as passing tones only. Strong beats must be consonant.

3rd Species — 4:1 (Quarter notes)

Four notes against each cantus note. Dissonances allowed as passing tones and neighbour tones on weak beats. Introduces anticipations and cambiatas.

4th Species — Syncopated (Suspensions)

The most important species. Notes are tied over the barline, creating dissonances on strong beats that resolve down by step. The 4-3, 7-6, and 9-8 suspensions are the foundation of all tonal voice leading.

5th Species — Florid (Free)

Combines all previous species freely. This is the synthesis — all note values, suspensions, and dissonance treatments combined into expressive, flowing counterpoint.

Study text: Johann Joseph Fux — "Gradus ad Parnassum" (1725) is the original counterpoint treatise, used by Haydn, Mozart, and Beethoven in their own studies.

The Deliberate Practice Framework

🧠 Ericsson's Deliberate Practice Principles (Applied to Violin)
  1. Work at the edge of your ability — always practice what is just beyond comfortable. Never coast.
  2. Immediate feedback loop — record every session, or use a mirror. Errors unheard are errors uncorrected.
  3. Isolate and drill — extract the difficult 4 measures. Practice ONLY those measures for 30 minutes. Do not run through the whole piece repeatedly.
  4. Slow → Fast — all new material begins at 50% of target tempo. Increase 4 BPM per day maximum when secure.
  1. Mental practice — 15 minutes of silent score study/visualisation per day activates the same neural pathways as physical practice.
  2. Rest is practice — the brain consolidates motor memories during sleep. 8 hours of sleep is non-negotiable for musicians.
  3. Galamian Rhythmic Variations — practice all passages in dotted rhythms (long-short, short-long) before playing evenly. This strengthens weak fingers.
  4. Practice the transitions — the bar before and after a difficulty is as important as the difficulty itself.

✍️ Melodic Dictation Method

Step-by-Step Dictation Process

  1. Listen once: Identify the key, time signature, and tempo only
  2. Listen twice: Identify the first and last note. Hear the overall shape (up, down, arching)
  3. Listen three times: Fill in the contour — what general direction is each phrase?
  4. Listen four times: Fill in specific pitches. Work from strong beats backward
  5. Listen five times: Rhythmic details, any ornaments
  6. Check: Sing your transcription back against the recording

Ear Training Drills by Level

Level I — Interval Recognition

Listen to two notes played consecutively. Identify: is the second note higher or lower? By how much? Start with perfect intervals only (4ths, 5ths, octaves).

Level I — Rhythm Reading

Clap back rhythms of increasing complexity. Start with quarter and half notes. Progress to eighth notes. Use a metronome and clap along before playing.

Level II — Scale Identification

Listen to a scale played ascending. Identify: major or minor? Which type of minor (natural/harmonic/melodic)? Pentatonic or modal?

Level II — Chord Quality

Listen to a chord (blocked or arpeggiated). Identify: major, minor, diminished, or augmented? Then: triad or seventh chord? What type of seventh?

Level III — Harmonic Dictation

Listen to a chord progression (4–8 bars). Write the Roman numeral analysis. Identify all cadences, secondary dominants, and borrowed chords.

Level III — Melodic Dictation

8-bar melody in major or minor keys. Write out the complete melody using the 5-listen method. Include all rhythmic values and accidentals.

Level IV — Transcription

Transcribe violin solos by ear (start with Suzuki pieces, advance to Kreutzer, then concerto excerpts). This is the ultimate ear training tool.

Level IV — Sight Singing

Sing back any melody immediately upon seeing the score. Use solfège. Practice with Bach chorales, then violin etudes, then orchestral excerpts.
